Magnesium is an essential mineral and cofactor in over 300 enzymatic reactions, including ATP production, DNA synthesis, neurotransmitter regulation, and vascular tone. It exists in the body predominantly inside cells and bone; serum magnesium represents less than 1% of total body stores and is therefore a poor marker of status except in severe deficiency. Red blood cell (RBC) magnesium provides a more reliable intracellular estimate.

Supplementation evidence is strongest for specific forms matched to specific indications. Magnesium glycinate (also labeled bisglycinate) combines magnesium with the calming amino acid glycine and has the best-documented effects for sleep quality and anxiety, typically at 300-400mg elemental daily taken evening. Magnesium L-threonate is the only form demonstrated to cross the blood-brain barrier and elevate cerebrospinal fluid magnesium in humans, making it preferred for cognitive indications at 1,500-2,000mg daily (delivering approximately 144mg elemental). Magnesium citrate is well-absorbed but causes loose stools at higher doses. Magnesium oxide should be avoided for most indications due to bioavailability near 4%.

Clinically relevant deficiency contributes to migraine frequency, muscle cramps, poor sleep architecture, premenstrual symptoms, and elevated blood pressure. Populations at elevated risk include adults on proton pump inhibitors, diuretics, or metformin; individuals with Crohn's disease or celiac disease; and heavy alcohol users. Supplementation is generally safe up to 350mg daily from supplements in adults without renal impairment, though higher doses (used in specific protocols) require slow titration to avoid gastrointestinal effects.
