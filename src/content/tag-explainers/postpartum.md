The postpartum period encompasses the physiological, neurological, and psychological transition following childbirth, with the first 12 months representing a distinct biological state rather than merely a recovery phase. Hormonal changes are dramatic: estradiol drops from approximately 15,000-30,000 pg/mL in pregnancy to near-premenopausal levels within 24-48 hours, and allopregnanolone (a progesterone metabolite with GABAergic activity) collapses. These shifts drive vulnerability to postpartum depression, anxiety, and cognitive symptoms collectively known as postpartum brain fog.

Nutritional depletion is nearly universal without intentional repletion. Pregnancy and lactation transfer substantial reserves of iron (often producing ferritin below 30 ng/mL), omega-3 DHA (depleted into fetal/infant neural tissue), choline (90-95% of postpartum women fail to meet 550mg daily requirements), B-vitamins, iodine, and vitamin D. These deficiencies contribute measurably to fatigue, mood symptoms, cognitive symptoms, and infant outcomes via breastmilk composition.

Evidence-based protocols prioritize biomarker-guided repletion over universal supplementation. Recommended testing includes ferritin, 25-hydroxyvitamin D, TSH/free T4 (postpartum thyroiditis affects 5-10%), and symptom screening with validated instruments (EPDS, GAD-7) at 2, 6, and 12 weeks minimum. Breastfeeding compatibility is well-established for most nutritional interventions per Hale's Medications and Mothers' Milk; some botanicals (St. John's Wort, certain adaptogens) should be avoided or used cautiously. Sleep fragmentation is unavoidable but contributes substantially to cognitive symptoms; family or community support that enables 4-hour consolidated sleep blocks produces measurable benefit.
