Recovery refers to the physiological restoration processes following stress, whether physical (exercise, injury, childbirth), psychological (burnout, trauma), or illness-related. It is a multifactorial process involving sleep, nutrition, inflammation resolution, tissue repair, and psychological regulation. Recovery is not a passive state but an active period during which adaptation occurs; inadequate recovery reduces benefit from the original stressor and can produce cumulative dysfunction.

Physical recovery from exercise depends on sleep quality and duration (7-9 hours with adequate slow-wave sleep for growth hormone release), adequate protein intake (1.6-2.2g/kg body weight daily with distribution across meals), sufficient total energy intake, and time between high-intensity sessions targeting the same muscle group (typically 24-72 hours). Active recovery (low-intensity movement) reduces perceived soreness faster than passive rest. Creatine monohydrate (5g daily) supports recovery, hydration, and performance maintenance during reduced training loads.

Postpartum recovery operates on longer timescales. The first 6-12 weeks involve acute tissue healing; the 6-12 month period involves nutritional repletion and hormonal re-equilibration; cognitive and structural brain recovery extends to 24 months or beyond. Recovery from burnout requires 8-12 weeks at minimum, often longer if environmental factors (workload, interpersonal conflict) are not addressed. Chronic overreaching in athletes or high-performing professionals may require 2-8 weeks of complete rest or substantial reduction plus nutrient repletion. Supplementation cannot compensate for inadequate sleep, nutrition, or psychological recovery conditions, but targeted interventions (omega-3 for inflammation, magnesium for sleep, adaptogens for HPA regulation) can accelerate recovery when foundational factors are addressed.
