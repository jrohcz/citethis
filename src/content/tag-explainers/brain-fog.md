Brain fog is a colloquial term describing a cluster of cognitive symptoms including slowed processing, difficulty concentrating, word-finding problems, short-term memory lapses, and mental fatigue. It is not a formal medical diagnosis but a recognizable presentation across multiple conditions: post-viral syndromes (including long COVID), postpartum recovery, perimenopausal transition, chronic sleep deprivation, nutrient deficiencies, thyroid dysfunction, and chronic stress or burnout.

Distinguishing brain fog from depression matters for intervention: brain fog without core affective symptoms (anhedonia, persistent low mood, hopelessness, anxiety) is typically a physiological rather than psychiatric presentation. Screening with validated tools (PHQ-9, GAD-7, EPDS for postpartum) should precede attribution to brain fog alone. Workup includes thyroid function (TSH, free T4), iron studies (ferritin, transferrin saturation), B12 with methylmalonic acid, 25-hydroxyvitamin D, basic metabolic panel, and sleep disorder screening.

Interventional evidence is driver-dependent. Postpartum brain fog responds to targeted nutrient repletion (omega-3 DHA, choline, iron if deficient, B-complex) and sleep optimization, with substantial recovery by 12 months and full recovery often by 24 months (Hoekzema et al., 2017). Post-viral cognitive symptoms have less established intervention evidence; modest support exists for creatine, omega-3, and graded activity. Cognitive symptoms during perimenopause respond partially to hormone replacement therapy and partially to targeted supplementation. Sleep regulation is the single highest-yield intervention across all brain fog presentations.
