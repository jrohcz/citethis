## 1. DIAGNOSTIKA — The diagnostic landscape and its blind spots

### Czech waiting times and the diagnostic gap

No centralized Czech data on adult ADHD waiting times exists as of 2026. Charles University's Centrum Carolina offers student pre-screening with approximately **6–8 week waits**, but this is not a full psychiatric diagnosis. For context, **61.6% of UK adults on ADHD waiting lists have waited over one year**, with some areas reporting 5–10 year waits (Asherson et al., 2024, *BJPsych Bulletin*, 48(1):1–5). Czech waitlists are likely substantial given the limited number of adult ADHD specialists, but remain unquantified in the published literature.

The most important Czech epidemiological study comes from Vňuková et al. (2021, *Journal of Attention Disorders*, 25(12):1657–1664), surveying **1,518 representative adults**. Only **3% reported ever receiving a formal ADHD diagnosis**, while **7.8% screened positive** on the ASRS. An earlier study by Weissenberger et al. (2018, *Neuropsychiatric Disease and Treatment*, 14:293–299) found **3.36%** elevated symptomatology. Globally, Song et al. (2021, *Journal of Global Health*, 11:04009) estimated persistent adult ADHD at **2.58%** and symptomatic adult ADHD at **6.76%**, representing roughly **366 million adults worldwide**. The true Czech diagnostic gap is likely far larger than the raw numbers suggest — the 3% "ever diagnosed" figure includes childhood diagnoses, and Weissenberger et al. (2022, *Frontiers in Psychology*, 13:799344) noted that "a lack of acceptance of ADHD as a lifelong disorder has generated many challenges" in Czech psychiatry. Adult ADHD diagnoses in the Czech Republic have increased **50-fold over the past 12 years** according to ÚZIS data, driven primarily by media attention (iROZHLAS, July 2023).

### Brown ADD Scales vs. ASRS v1.1

The Brown Attention-Deficit Disorder Scales (revised in 2018 as **Brown EF/A Scales**) offer a **40-item assessment across 5 executive function clusters**: organizing/activating, sustaining attention, sustaining energy/effort, managing affective interference, and utilizing working memory. The Brazilian validation (Da Silva et al., 2023, *São Paulo Medical Journal*, 141(3):e2022219) reported **Cronbach's α = 0.95**, sensitivity of **72%**, and specificity of **88%**. Brown et al. (2022, *Journal of Attention Disorders*, 26(2):256–266) demonstrated treatment sensitivity across all 5 clusters.

The key advantage over ASRS v1.1 is conceptual scope. ASRS is built strictly on DSM symptom criteria (18 items, 6-item screener), while Brown captures **executive dysfunction domains not in the DSM** — emotional regulation, energy/effort, and working memory. This makes Brown scales significantly better at detecting ADHD in high-functioning adults and women who have compensated for core DSM symptoms but still experience significant EF impairment. ASRS has an AUC of **0.904** (Brevik et al., 2020, *Brain and Behavior*, 10(6):e01605) and high negative predictive value (**98%**) for ruling out ADHD (Shah & Sultan, 2025, *Indian Journal of Psychological Medicine*), but produces high false-positive rates. **No Czech validation of the Brown scales was identified** — only the Czech ASRS has been formally validated (Weissenberger et al., 2022, *Frontiers in Psychology*, 13:799344).

### Compensatory mechanisms and screening false negatives

High-functioning adults develop elaborate compensatory strategies that systematically lower their scores on screening questionnaires, creating false negatives. Canela et al. (2017, *PLOS ONE*, 12(9):e0184964) classified strategies into 5 categories — organizational, motoric, attentional, social, and psychopharmacological — among 32 ADHD outpatients. Palmieri et al. (2022, *Frontiers in Psychology*, 13:1015102) found that **advancing age and higher education** were associated with better cognitive test performance in ADHD patients, suggesting progressive compensatory development. The Compensatory ADHD Behaviors Scale (CABS), developed by Castagna et al. (2019, *Assessment*, 26(8):1523–1537), was the first validated instrument for measuring these behaviors. Shah & Sultan (2025) explicitly stated: "Satisfactory academic achievement should not rule out an ADHD diagnosis, especially in females, due to the use of compensatory strategies."

### Gender diagnostic gap, masking, and late diagnosis

The childhood clinical diagnosis ratio stands at roughly **3:1 to 9:1 male:female**, but population-based studies suggest a true prevalence ratio closer to **2:1** (Martin, 2024, *Lancet Psychiatry*). In adulthood, the ratio narrows to approximately **1:1**, confirming massive childhood underdiagnosis in girls. Young et al. (2024, *Psychological Medicine*, 54(14):3763–3784) meta-analyzed 52 studies (8,423 females, 9,985 males) and found males had significantly more severe hyperactivity-impulsivity on rating scales, with **no significant sex differences in inattention**. Between 2007–2016, diagnoses in **adult women rose 344%** versus 264% for men (JAMA Network Open).

Liang et al. (2023, *Journal of Attention Disorders*, 27(6):645–657) reported that **14% of girls** with ADHD were prescribed antidepressants before any ADHD treatment, versus only **5% of boys**. Women are typically not diagnosed until their **30s or 40s**, often triggered by their children's diagnosis. Masking mechanisms include suppressing hyperactive behaviors, over-preparing to compensate for inattention, mimicking neurotypical social behaviors, and creating elaborate organizational systems. Consequences include higher rates of anxiety, depression, burnout, chronic low self-esteem, and a grief reaction upon late diagnosis described as "mourning the lost years." King's College London is developing the **Masking ADHD School Questionnaire (MASQ)**, the first validated masking measurement tool.

### Hormonal influences and the estrogen-dopamine axis

Roberts, Martel & Nigg (2018, *Psychoneuroendocrinology*, 88:105–114) tracked 32 women daily for 35 days with saliva hormone samples and found that **decreased estradiol in the context of increased progesterone or testosterone predicted ADHD symptoms the following day**, with clinically significant **2-fold increases** in symptoms. Martel & Roberts (2024, *Hormones and Behavior*, 158:105478) proposed the **"double whammy" theory**: rapid estrogen declines decrease executive function at two cycle points — post-ovulatory (increasing hyperactivity-impulsivity) and perimenstrual (increasing inattention). Kautzky et al. (2025, *Journal of Clinical Medicine*, 15(1):121) reviewed 29 studies and proposed two mechanistic pathways: an estrogen-withdrawal model affecting dopaminergic signaling and an allopregnanolone-sensitivity model involving altered GABAergic modulation.

The mechanism is well-characterized: **estrogen increases dopamine synthesis, receptor density, and limits reuptake** through inhibition of monoamine oxidase (Osianlis et al., 2025, *Journal of Attention Disorders*, 29(9):706–723). High estrogen phases (follicular, pregnancy) enhance dopamine signaling and reduce ADHD symptoms; low-estrogen phases (late luteal, postpartum, menopause) worsen them. Postpartum data show a **5-fold higher risk for postpartum depression** in mothers with ADHD versus those without (Massachusetts General Hospital, 2023). Wynchank et al. (2023, *Frontiers in Psychiatry*, 14:1306194) piloted cyclical upward adjustment of stimulant dosage premenstrually in 9 women, showing symptom improvement without increased side effects. **⚠ FLAG: Much of the estrogen-dopamine-ADHD evidence comes from animal models, case studies, and small pilot studies. Large-scale RCTs are lacking.**

## 2. FARMAKOLOGIE — Medications, interactions, and Czech regulatory barriers

### Atomoxetine efficacy and dosing

The NNT for atomoxetine treatment response is **3.43** (95% CI: 2.79–4.45; Cheng et al., 2007, *Psychopharmacology*, 194(2):197–209). In adults, the effect size versus placebo is SMD **−0.45** (Cunill et al., 2016, *Journal of Psychopharmacology*). By comparison, the landmark Cortese et al. (2018, *Lancet Psychiatry*, 5(9):714–726) network meta-analysis found amphetamines significantly more efficacious (SMD **−0.79**) than atomoxetine in adults. Approximately **40% of patients continue to have significant symptomatology** even on atomoxetine. The target adult dose is **80–100 mg/day** (1.2 mg/kg/day), titrated from 40 mg/day over 3–7 days; full effect requires 1–4 weeks. CYP2D6 poor metabolizers or those on fluoxetine/paroxetine require dose adjustment.

### Lisdexamfetamine vs. methylphenidate

Lisdexamfetamine (LDX) is an inactive prodrug conjugated to L-lysine, cleaved enzymatically primarily in red blood cells — not the GI tract — to release active d-amphetamine (Soutullo et al., 2013, *CNS Drugs*, 27:S1–S11). T-max is **3.4–3.6 hours**, half-life **8.6–8.9 hours**, with **≥14 hours duration of action** in adults. Head-to-head data from Newcorn et al. (2017, *JAACAP*) showed LDX superior to OROS-methylphenidate in forced-dose studies (treatment difference −3.4, p=0.0013, ES −0.33), though flexible-dose studies showed only a non-significant trend (ES −0.20, p=0.072). **⚠ Note: Key comparative studies were Shire-funded, creating potential conflicts of interest.**

### Combination therapy: atomoxetine + stimulant

Cheng & Boileau (2025, *Cureus*, 17(2):e79378) updated the systematic review with 6 retrospective studies from 2012–2024 and concluded that "some but not all patients may benefit." Rationale includes extending symptom coverage beyond stimulant wearing-off, allowing lower stimulant doses, and addressing partial responders. Safety data show no serious adverse events across reviews, and no clinically significant pharmacokinetic interaction between atomoxetine and methylphenidate. **⚠ FLAG: No large prospective RCT has evaluated this combination. All evidence is from heterogeneous, mostly retrospective, small studies. This combination is off-label everywhere.**

### SSRI misdiagnosis pattern

A Finnish nationwide register study (Prami et al., 2025, *Acta Psychiatrica Scandinavica*, via PMC12318640) examining **66,146 ADHD patients** found that many adults had been prescribed antidepressants before ADHD diagnosis; after initiating ADHD treatment, antidepressant use **dropped significantly** while controls showed no change. Powell et al. (2021, *Journal of Affective Disorders*) found **12.8%** of recurrently depressed women had elevated ADHD symptoms — **none had been diagnosed**. An estimated **34% of patients with treatment-resistant depression** had previously undetected ADHD (Sternat & Katzman). **⚠ FLAG: No single study gives a precise percentage of ADHD adults receiving SSRI as first-line; the pattern is well-supported directionally but hard to quantify precisely.**

### Vitamin C and amphetamine excretion

The mechanism is pharmacologically robust: amphetamine is a weak base (pKa ~9.9); in acidic urine, it becomes protonated and cannot be passively reabsorbed in renal tubules, increasing excretion. Beckett & Rowland (1965, *Journal of Pharmacy and Pharmacology*, 17:628–639) showed urinary excretion increased up to **11-fold** when urine pH decreased from ~8.0 to ~5.0. Huang & Isoherranen (2020, *Journal of Pharmacology and Experimental Therapeutics*, 373(3):488–501) confirmed via PBPK modeling that alkaline urine decreased amphetamine excretion by **91–97%**. However, the **clinical significance at normal dietary vitamin C doses (100–500 mg) is debated** — achieving meaningful sustained urinary acidification requires very high doses (>2 g). Lisdexamfetamine's bloodstream conversion makes it less susceptible to GI pH effects, though urinary pH effects on the active d-amphetamine metabolite still apply. The FDA Adderall label lists ascorbic acid as a GI acidifying agent that lowers absorption.

### L-tyrosine: no evidence supports use

The only ADHD-specific clinical trial (Reimherr et al., 1987, *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 144(8):1071–3) tested L-tyrosine at 50–150 mg/kg/day in 12 adults: **8 showed initial improvement, but all 8 developed tolerance by 6 weeks**. A pediatric double-blind study (Nemzer et al., 1986) found tyrosine **not different from placebo**. Bergwerff et al. (2016, *PLOS ONE*, 11(3):e0151100) found no tyrosine abnormalities in ADHD children versus controls. The theoretical rationale (tyrosine as dopamine precursor replenishing stores depleted by stimulants) is undermined by the fact that tyrosine hydroxylase is regulated by end-product inhibition — simply increasing substrate does not proportionally increase synthesis. **⚠ FLAG: There is no high-quality evidence supporting L-tyrosine for ADHD treatment or as a stimulant adjunct. Claims on supplement websites are not evidence-based.**

### Neuroinflammation and oxidative stress

Schiweck et al. (2023, *Translational Psychiatry*) assessed 96 inflammatory proteins in 126 adult ADHD participants and identified **two distinct biotypes**: Higher Inflammatory Potential (HIP) and Lower Inflammatory Potential (LIP), with NF-κB and chemokine signaling pathways differing between groups. Oxidative stress markers (elevated MDA, reduced total antioxidant status) are more consistently found in ADHD (Corona, 2020, *Antioxidants*, 9(11):1039), though data remains heterogeneous. Current consensus: **neuroinflammation characterizes a subgroup of ADHD patients, not all**; the association is established but **causal role is NOT established**. Methylphenidate may partially correct the oxidative profile (Garre-Morata et al., 2024, *Antioxidants*, 13:92).

### Why stimulants are not first-line in Czech Republic

**Atomoxetine is the only drug with an explicit approved adult ADHD indication** for new treatment initiation in the Czech Republic (Mohr et al., 2012, ČNPS Consensus; Masopust et al., 2014, *Psychiatrie pro Praxi*, 15(3):112–117). Methylphenidate (Concerta) was recently approved for adults by SÚKL but is "not intended for initiating treatment in adults" — it should continue treatment from adolescence. Ritalin (IR methylphenidate) is **not approved for adults** at all. Stimulants are classified as controlled psychotropic substances requiring a **blue-stripe prescription** (recept s modrým pruhem) and can only be prescribed by psychiatrists. The regulatory caution stems from three factors: (1) the historical Czech methamphetamine ("pervitin") problem making authorities wary of amphetamine-class drugs; (2) European regulatory conservatism; and (3) atomoxetine's lack of abuse potential. The Updated European Consensus (Kooij et al., 2019, *European Psychiatry*, 56:14–34) noted that "the hesitancy about stimulant medication may also be related to the history of abuse with amphetamines in the past."

## 3. DOPLŇKY — Supplements ranked by evidence strength

### Magnesium: moderate evidence for deficiency, limited trial data

Two meta-analyses confirmed lower magnesium in ADHD: Huang et al. (2019, *Progress in Neuro-Psychopharmacology and Biological Psychiatry*) found serum magnesium **Hedges' g = −0.733** lower in ADHD children across 8 studies. Regarding forms, magnesium oxide has fractional absorption of approximately **4%** (Firoz & Graber, 2001), while glycinate/bisglycinate achieves **~50–80%** absorption partly via dipeptide transporter pathways (Schuette et al., 1994, *Journal of the American College of Nutrition*, 13(5):429–435). **Magnesium L-threonate is the only form with ADHD-specific trial data**: Surman et al. (2021, *Journal of Dietary Supplements*, 18(2):119–131) conducted an open-label pilot in 15 adults, with **47% meeting response criteria** — but this was unblinded, n=15, and industry-funded (Neurocentria Inc.). L-threonate uniquely crosses the blood-brain barrier and increases brain magnesium in hippocampus and PFC (preclinical evidence). Lopresti et al. (2026, *Frontiers in Nutrition*, 12) showed cognitive improvement with 2g/day Magtein® in 100 adults over 6 weeks, though not ADHD-specific. Studies used **6 mg/kg/day elemental magnesium** in children.

### Omega-3: small and inconsistent effects

The most comprehensive recent meta-analysis (Chang et al., 2023, *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry*, 84(5): 22 RCTs, 1,789 participants) found omega-3 PUFAs **did not significantly improve core ADHD symptoms** overall (SMD: −0.16; 95% CI: −0.34 to 0.01; P = .07). However, the subgroup with treatment **≥4 months** showed significant benefit (SMD: **−0.35**; P = .007). Notably, **neither high EPA dosage nor high EPA:DHA ratio improved outcomes** in this analysis, contradicting earlier findings by Bloch & Qawasmi (2011, *JAACAP*). A 2025 systematic review in *Principles and Practice of Clinical Research* concluded: "Omega-3 may not have a significant effect on ADHD symptoms to recommend its use." **Evidence strength: WEAK-TO-MODERATE** — small effect sizes, not consistently significant.

### Ferritin: a meaningful biomarker with clear mechanism

The landmark Konofal et al. (2004, *Archives of Pediatrics & Adolescent Medicine*, 158(12):1113–5) study found mean ferritin of **23 ng/mL in ADHD children versus 44 ng/mL in controls** (P < .001), with **84% of ADHD children below 30 ng/mL**. Ferritin inversely correlated with ADHD severity (r = −0.34). Tseng et al. (2018, *Scientific Reports*) meta-analyzed 17 articles and confirmed significantly lower ferritin (Hedges' g = **−0.246**) and dramatically higher ADHD severity in iron-deficient children (Hedges' g = **0.888**). The mechanism is clear: **iron is a cofactor for tyrosine hydroxylase**, the rate-limiting enzyme converting tyrosine to L-DOPA in dopamine synthesis. The threshold most commonly cited for concern is **<30 ng/mL** (Konofal et al., 2004, 2008), though some clinicians cite <45–50 ng/mL for optimal brain function — this higher threshold lacks rigorous RCT validation. Supplementation trials (Konofal et al., 2008; Panahandeh et al., 2017) show improvement in ferritin-deficient ADHD children.

### Zinc: moderate evidence as DAT modulator

Zinc is a **non-competitive blocker of the dopamine transporter (DAT)** at a high-affinity extracellular binding site (His-193, His-375, Glu-396), inhibiting reuptake and increasing carrier-mediated dopamine efflux (Lepping & Huber, 2010, *CNS Neuroscience & Therapeutics*). Talebi et al. (2022, *Critical Reviews in Food Science and Nutrition*) meta-analyzed 6 RCTs (489 children) and found zinc supplementation **significantly reduced ADHD total scores** (SMD: **−0.62**; P = .04), though individual subscales (hyperactivity, inattention) were not individually significant. Bilici et al. (2004) tested zinc sulfate 150 mg/day in 400 children and found significant improvement. However, Skalny et al. (2021, *Scientific Reports*: 22 studies) found **no statistically significant difference** in serum zinc between ADHD and controls in the primary analysis, though sensitivity analysis did show lower zinc in ADHD.

### Vitamin D: strong association, notable Czech deficiency

A 2025 meta-analysis in *Middle East Current Psychiatry* found ADHD children have **−6.55 ng/mL lower serum vitamin D** (P < .001), with **OR 1.97** for ADHD in deficient children. Vitamin D regulates **tyrosine hydroxylase expression** (Cui et al., 2015, *Neuroscience*, 304:90–100), directly linking it to dopamine synthesis. Czech-specific data is alarming: Holmannová et al. (2025, *European Journal of Clinical Nutrition*, 79(7):641–652) studying **119,925 Czechs** found sufficiency (≥75 nmol/L) in only **19.2% of 6–15-year-olds** and **22.1% of 16–30-year-olds**. Dietary intake is inadequate in **>95% of the Czech population** aged 4+ (Czech TDS, 2018, *Nutrients*, 10(10):1452), and only **13.5% supplement year-round** while **51.5% never supplement** (2025, *Nutrients*, 17(16):2623). Supplementation meta-analyses show benefit as adjunctive therapy to methylphenidate but low overall strength of evidence.

### Creatine and B6: theoretical rationale but minimal ADHD evidence

**No dedicated ADHD RCTs for creatine exist.** Wu et al. (2024, *Frontiers in Nutrition*, 11: 16 RCTs, 492 participants) showed creatine improved **memory** (SMD = 0.31) and **attention time** (SMD = −0.31) in general populations but not executive function. The EFSA Panel (2024) explicitly concluded that a cause-and-effect relationship between creatine and cognitive improvement "has NOT been established." The rationale (brain ATP regeneration via phosphocreatine) is plausible but unvalidated in ADHD. **Evidence strength: WEAK for ADHD.**

For **B6 (P-5-P)**: the biochemical role is textbook fact — pyridoxal 5'-phosphate is the essential cofactor for **DOPA decarboxylase** (aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase), catalyzing both L-DOPA → dopamine and 5-HTP → serotonin (Safo et al., 2023, *International Journal of Molecular Sciences*, 24(1):642). Landaas et al. (2016, *BJPsych Open*) found B6 significantly lower in 131 ADHD adults. Cracknell et al. (2024, *Journal of Psychopharmacology*) found high-dose B6 (100 mg) reduced sensory over-responsivity in 300 adults, relevant because SOR co-occurs in **22–43% of ADHD**. But **no RCT of B6 alone for ADHD exists**. The biochemistry is strong; the clinical ADHD evidence is weak.

## 4. ČESKÝ SYSTÉM — Navigating Czech adult ADHD care

### How diagnosis works and who can provide it

An ADHD diagnosis in the Czech Republic can be made by a **psychiatrist** or **clinical psychologist** (klinický psycholog), but medication requires a psychiatrist's prescription. Diagnosis follows **ICD-10/ICD-11 criteria** (code F90.0: Porucha aktivity a pozornosti). The validated Czech ASRS translation (Weissenberger et al., 2022, *Frontiers in Psychology*) is used for screening; WURS for retrospective childhood assessment; and DIVA (Czech translation exists) for structured diagnostic interview, though "it is unfortunately not yet common practice among clinicians." Computerized attention tests are used by some psychologists but are **not covered by public health insurance** (surcharge ~2,000 CZK). Adults must demonstrate persistence of ADHD symptoms from childhood, ideally confirmed by parental interview, school records, or structured instruments (iROZHLAS, July 2023, citing psychiatrist Jiří Renka, NUDZ Klecany).

### Medication availability: a constrained market

The Czech stimulant market is significantly more limited than Western Europe. Key facts:

- **Atomoxetine** (Strattera, generics Atofab, Atominex, Bitixen): The **only medication approved for initiating adult ADHD treatment** in CZ. Covered by insurance with copay ~**1,825 CZK/month** for 80mg (lekyjasne.cz). Specialist-only prescription required.
- **Concerta** (OROS methylphenidate): SÚKL recently expanded registration to adults, but it is "not intended for initiating treatment in adults" — only continuation from adolescence. Blue-stripe (controlled substance) prescription required. Copay approximately **221 CZK** per 30 tablets at 18mg (can be 0 CZK with enhanced reimbursement). **Supply issues since December 2024.**
- **Ritalin** (IR methylphenidate): **Not approved for adults** in CZ.
- **Elvanse** (lisdexamfetamine): Practically unavailable through normal channels — requires **special import (mimořádný dovoz)** with ~3-month delivery and cost of ~**12,000 CZK/package**, fully out-of-pocket (Dr. Max pharmacy).
- **Livizux** (lisdexamfetamine by Egis): Registered since 2024, undergoing pricing/reimbursement procedures. Actual market availability uncertain.
- **Adderall**: Not available and never approved in Europe.
- A **patient petition** on petice.com demands wider availability of lisdexamfetamine and amphetamine formulations.

### Insurance coverage and costs

Zaměstnanecká pojišťovna Škoda (ZPŠ) explicitly confirmed in 2024 that both Concerta and Strattera are reimbursed for adults when prescriptive requirements are met, "also when diagnosed in adulthood, subject to approved Form No. 21." Psychotherapy for ADHD is also covered. However, information is inconsistent between insurance companies, and some may still apply older rules.

Representative private costs (AdiCare, Prague): initial diagnostic consultation **1,790 CZK** (50 min); targeted clinical-psychological diagnosis with report **5,950 CZK** (4–5 hours); initial psychiatric examination **3,190 CZK**; follow-up psychiatric appointment **1,490–1,890 CZK**. The public pathway covers psychiatric consultations and partially covers psychological examinations through insurance, but wait times drive many patients to private clinics. CDR Beroun offers a **12-day residential diagnostic-educational program** — healthcare covered by insurance, accommodation **25,200 CZK** total.

### Specialized centers and practitioners

Key resources include **AdiCare** (Prague, Brno, Litoměřice — private ADHD/ADD diagnosis and CBT programs), **NUDZ Klecany** (National Institute of Mental Health — research and clinical), **KAPPA Praha** (MUDr. Holčapková, adult ADHD), **nepozornidospeli.cz** (patient resource site by Mgr. Štěpánková, Ph.D.), and **adhdstrategie.cz** (translated strategies and practitioner directory). Key academic researchers: **Prof. Radek Ptáček** and **Dr. Simon Weissenberger** (Charles University), **Dr. Michal Goetz** (Motol), **MUDr. Jiří Renka** (NUDZ).

### Legal protections and disability

ADHD does not have specific adult disability legislation in Czech law. Disability benefits are assessed based on **functional impairment** under zákon č. 155/1995 Sb. (Pension Insurance Act), with three invalidity levels (35–49%, 50–69%, ≥70% working capacity reduction). ADHD alone is unlikely to qualify for higher degrees unless severely impairing and treatment-resistant, but cases with significant comorbidities may qualify. The Anti-Discrimination Act (zákon č. 198/2009 Sb.) prohibits employment discrimination on disability grounds. Persons recognized as "osoba se zdravotním postižením" are entitled to workplace accommodations, and employers receive tax incentives (18,000–60,000 CZK/year). Medical diagnosis is **confidential** — employers cannot demand disclosure.

## 5. SELF-MEDIKACE — From substances to evidence-based treatment

### The scope of self-medication

**50% of young adults with ADHD** have had a lifetime substance use disorder, compared to 23.6% without ADHD (Fuller-Thomson et al., 2023, *Alcohol and Alcoholism*). An estimated **21% of adults in substance abuse treatment** have comorbid ADHD (Pekrul et al., 2023, *International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health*). **70% of young adults** with dual ADHD-SUD report using substances to self-medicate rather than to get high (RehabsUK). The Khantzian self-medication hypothesis (1985, *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 142(11):1259–1264) proposes that drug choice is not random — individuals select substances whose pharmacologic action addresses their dominant painful affects. Most commonly used: alcohol (36% with AUD), cannabis (23% with CUD), nicotine, cocaine (paradoxical calming reported), and amphetamines.

### Caffeine: promising animal data, sparse human evidence

Caffeine blocks adenosine A1 and A2A receptors, indirectly increasing dopamine and norepinephrine release in the prefrontal cortex — the same neurotransmitters targeted by ADHD medications. Vázquez et al. (2022, *Nutrients*, 14(4):739) found caffeine **increased attention, reduced locomotor activity, and improved learning** in animal ADHD models. The "paradoxical calming" reaction is widely reported anecdotally, and Grünblatt & Walitza (2011, *Deutsches Ärzteblatt International*) noted that "many people with ADHD react paradoxically to caffeine." However, Perrotte et al. (2023, *Brain Sciences*, 13(9):1304) found only 5 human RCTs meeting inclusion criteria, with inconclusive results. **⚠ FLAG: The paradoxical calming effect is clinically observed but NOT systematically validated in controlled human trials. Caffeine is not a substitute for evidence-based treatment.**

### Nicotine: strong evidence for self-medication mechanism

Adults with ADHD smoke at **41–43%** versus ~14–19% of the general population (McClernon & Kollins, 2008, *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 1141:131–147). Nicotine activates **α4β2 nicotinic acetylcholine receptors** in the VTA and PFC, triggering dopamine release. Potter et al. (2006) found nicotine patches in nonsmoking ADHD adolescents improved cognitive/behavioral inhibition comparably to methylphenidate. Critically, Tripp & Wickens (2022, *Frontiers in Neuroscience*, 16:845646) found that **MAO-inhibitory compounds in tobacco smoke** (not just nicotine) provide dual dopamine enhancement — MAO-B inhibition plus nicotinic receptor activation. Van Amsterdam et al. (2018, *Substance Use & Misuse*) applied Hill's causality criteria and concluded self-medication is "very likely to play a causal role" in elevated ADHD smoking rates. Levin et al. (2010, *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*) showed that ADHD smokers with greater ADHD improvement on methylphenidate achieved **70% smoking abstinence** versus 10% on placebo.

### Self-medication versus substance abuse: clinical distinction

Self-medication is characterized by goal-directed use (improving concentration, calming hyperactivity), functional dosing, use tied to symptom states rather than social context, and relief rather than euphoria. SUD features loss of control, continued use despite harm, escalating tolerance, and functional impairment from the substance itself. The distinction is critical but often blurred. Clinicians should assess symptoms **during past periods of abstinence** and prior to substance use onset (Mariani et al., *PMC2676785*). The Updated European Consensus (Kooij et al., 2019) recommends targeting SUD as a primary disorder while treating ADHD in parallel. Critically, the VA Evidence Synthesis Program (Rieke et al., November 2024) found **no evidence that stimulant prescriptions increase SUD risk**. Treatment should NOT be deferred indefinitely awaiting abstinence — treating ADHD may help patients stop self-medicating. Long-acting formulations (OROS-methylphenidate, lisdexamfetamine) are strongly preferred for patients with SUD history.

### Kratom: complex pharmacology, significant risks

Kratom's primary alkaloid mitragynine is a partial mu-opioid agonist with alpha-adrenergic, serotonergic, and dopaminergic effects. At **low doses (1–5g)**, it produces stimulant-like effects; at **higher doses (5–15g)**, sedative/opioid effects (Singh et al., 2016, *Brain Research Bulletin*). Regular use leads to tolerance; in a Malaysian study of 293 regular users, **>50% developed severe dependence** (Singh et al., 2014, *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*). Withdrawal onset is 6–12 hours, peaking at 24–72 hours, with both physical (muscle aches, insomnia, sweating, nausea) and psychological symptoms (anxiety, cravings). Smith et al. (2022, *Journal of Addiction Medicine*, 16(6):666–670) found **29.5%** of ever-regular users met criteria for Kratom Use Disorder. One case report describes a 20-year-old with ADHD developing kratom use disorder after stopping prescribed stimulants (PubMed 31644379). **⚠ FLAG: No controlled studies examining kratom efficacy for ADHD exist. Products are unregulated with variable alkaloid concentrations. There is no evidence supporting kratom as safe or effective for ADHD.**

## 6. NOVÉ VÝZKUMY — Frontiers in ADHD science, 2024–2026

### Genetics: from candidate genes to polygenic architecture

The largest published GWAS (Demontis et al., 2023, *Nature Genetics*, 55(2):198–208) identified **27 genome-wide significant loci** across 38,691 ADHD cases and 186,843 controls, with **76 potential risk genes** enriched in early brain development and frontal cortex expression. A preliminary 2025 ECNP presentation expanded this to **>70,000 cases and >650,000 controls**, identifying **56 loci** in the first multi-ancestry analysis. The historically important DAT1 10-repeat and DRD4 7-repeat alleles have **not been replicated at genome-wide significance** in modern GWAS — they are now understood as having very small effect sizes within a highly polygenic architecture. Scassellati et al. (2020, *Translational Psychiatry*) meta-analysis found DRD4 7R as a risk factor specifically in European populations (OR=1.31), but this is modest. Polygenic risk scores explain modest variance and **lack clinical-level specificity** due to high genetic overlap — remarkably, **84–98% of ADHD-influencing variants are shared with other psychiatric disorders** (Demontis et al., 2023).

### Gut-brain axis: emerging but inconsistent

Dias et al. (2025, *Journal of Psychiatric Research*: 14 studies, 1,319 participants) found higher *Ruminococcus gnavus* and decreased *Faecalibacterium* (anti-inflammatory) in ADHD. Proposed mechanisms include altered neurotransmitter production, reduced short-chain fatty acids, and gut-mediated neuroinflammation. A 2025 meta-analysis of 15 probiotic RCTs (*Psychology, Health & Medicine*) found ADHD showed **greater improvement than ASD** with gut interventions (SMD = −0.24), with optimal duration of **8 weeks**. However, alpha and beta diversity findings are inconsistent, no single bacterial strain has been validated, and effect sizes are small. A novel "neurointestinal syndrome" hypothesis was proposed in 2025 (*Frontiers in Neuroscience*).

### Sleep and circadian disruption as core ADHD features

Luu & Fabiano (2025, *Frontiers in Psychiatry*, 16:1697900) synthesized converging evidence that ADHD may be fundamentally a **circadian rhythm disorder**: up to **78% of adults** with ADHD show delayed sleep-wake timing, with **dim-light melatonin onset (DLMO) delayed ~90 minutes** versus controls. Biological evidence includes reduced pineal gland volume, attenuated BMAL1/PER2 clock-gene rhythms, and blunted cortisol patterns. Kooij et al. (2021, *Chronobiology International*) showed 0.5 mg melatonin advanced DLMO by **1 hour 28 minutes** and reduced ADHD symptoms by **14%** in 51 adults with ADHD + DSPS, though sleep timing did not advance without behavioral coaching. A UK Delphi consensus (Asherson et al., 2025, *Frontiers in Psychiatry*, 16:1566390) reached **99% agreement** that ADHD adults with delayed sleep onset need pharmacological treatment access and **91% consensus** for melatonin when non-pharmacological treatments fail.

### Digital therapeutics: clinical promise, commercial failure

EndeavorRx received FDA De Novo clearance in 2020 (ages 8–12), 510(k) expansion to ages 8–17 in 2023, and **EndeavorOTC for adults was FDA-authorized June 2024**. In adult trials, **83% reported improved focus** and **32.7% showed ≥30% ADHD-RS reduction**. However, developer Akili Interactive experienced severe commercial struggles — revenue of only $323,000 in 2022, three rounds of layoffs, and was **acquired by Virtual Therapeutics for $34 million** in July 2024 (down from ~$1 billion IPO valuation). **No European regulatory approval** for ADHD digital therapeutics was identified. The clinical evidence is moderate and specific — real but modest improvements on objective attention measures that may not translate to behavioral symptoms.

### Executive dysfunction: the inverted-U confirmed and refined

Narayanan et al. (2022, *Neuropsychopharmacology*: meta-analysis of 75 studies) quantitatively confirmed the **inverted-U relationship** between prefrontal dopamine/D1 receptor levels and working memory — data best fit by a negative quadratic function. Manza et al. (2025, *PNAS*) found that the **D1:D2 receptor ratio** (not dopamine release magnitude) predicts methylphenidate response, fundamentally reshaping understanding of individual stimulant variability. Inagaki et al. (2025, *FEBS Journal*, 292(2):426–444) identified **NCX3** (sodium-calcium exchanger 3) in VTA dopaminergic neurons as a novel ADHD candidate — NCX3 knockdown disrupted CaMKII-DAT interaction and dopamine clearance in PFC, with all deficits ameliorated by methylphenidate.

## 7. BIOCHEMISTRY VERIFICATION — Fact-checking the core claims

### "Vacuum cleaner" metaphor for DAT/NET/SERT: partially accurate

**Verdict: OVERSIMPLIFIED.** DAT, NET, and SERT are Na⁺/Cl⁻-dependent **secondary active symporters** using the transmembrane sodium gradient (maintained by Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase) to transport neurotransmitters against their concentration gradient. DAT co-transports 1 dopamine with **2 Na⁺ and 1 Cl⁻** via an alternating-access mechanism (Cheng & Bahar, 2015, *Structure*, 23(11):2171–2181). The "vacuum cleaner" analogy correctly captures the primary function of clearing neurotransmitters from the synapse (DAT clears ~70% of striatal dopamine). However, it misleads in three ways: (1) these transporters are **selective, saturable, and energy-dependent** with Michaelis-Menten kinetics, not passive suction; (2) they can **operate in reverse** — amphetamine causes DAT to efflux dopamine outward; (3) the crucial ion-coupling mechanism is obscured. A better analogy: a **revolving door powered by a water wheel** (the Na⁺ gradient powers the door's rotation, carrying neurotransmitter through).

### Atomoxetine's dual NE + DA effect in PFC: accurate

**Verdict: ACCURATE.** Bymaster et al. (2002, *Neuropsychopharmacology*, 27:699–711) demonstrated that atomoxetine increases extracellular NE and DA each approximately **3-fold in the prefrontal cortex** while producing **no significant DA change in striatum or nucleus accumbens**. The mechanism: PFC has minimal DAT expression (Sesack et al., 1998, *Journal of Neuroscience*, 18:2697–2708), so NET handles dopamine reuptake there. By blocking NET, atomoxetine prevents clearance of both catecholamines. Carboni et al. (1990, *Journal of Neurochemistry*, 55:1067–1070) provided foundational evidence for this. The effect is primarily PFC-specific because other brain regions have abundant DAT to handle dopamine clearance independently.

### Paradoxical stimulant response: the "paradox" framing is outdated

**Verdict: The "paradox" framing is INACCURATE; the corrected explanation is ACCURATE.** Rapoport et al. (1978, *Science*, 199:560–563) demonstrated that stimulants produce similar cognitive-enhancing and activity-reducing effects in **normal children** — the response is not ADHD-specific. Arnsten (2006, *Neuropsychopharmacology*, 31:2376–2383) explained: "Low doses of stimulants focus attention and improve executive function in both normal and ADHD subjects." The current model is the **inverted-U dose-response curve**: ADHD individuals operate on the left (understimulated) side; stimulants shift them toward the optimal peak. Kay et al. (2025, *Cell*) showed stimulants primarily affect arousal and reward/salience networks similarly regardless of ADHD diagnosis. Mergy et al. (2022, *Scientific Reports*) confirmed that elevated PFC dopamine via NET blockade is the common calming mechanism.

### Hardware vs. Software treatment concept: clinically useful but oversimplified

**Verdict: OVERSIMPLIFIED but CLINICALLY USEFUL.** The MTA Study (MTA Cooperative Group, 1999, *Archives of General Psychiatry*, 56(12):1073–1086) supports the dual-track approach: medication was superior for core ADHD symptoms, while combined treatment showed advantages for non-ADHD outcomes (oppositional behavior, social skills, parent-child relations, academic performance). Normalization rates: combined **68%**, medication alone **56%**, behavioral therapy alone **34%**. The AAP (2019) recommends both medication and behavioral therapy together for children 6+. However, the metaphor is neuroscientifically imprecise because: (1) behavioral interventions physically change brain structure and function (neuroplasticity); (2) medication enhances neuroplasticity and learning capacity, not just "hardware"; (3) the brain is not a digital computer with separable hardware and software. A better metaphor: **medication is corrective lenses** (enabling clear vision) while behavioral therapy **teaches you how to read** (a skill requiring both vision and instruction). Or: medication prepares the neurochemical **"soil"** so behavioral strategies (the **"seeds"**) can take root.

## Conclusion

Three findings stand out as most actionable for the ADHD Kompas project:

1. The **Czech diagnostic infrastructure for adult ADHD is fundamentally underdeveloped** — atomoxetine is the only drug approved for adult treatment initiation, stimulant access is severely restricted, and no national waiting-time data even exists.

2. **The gender and compensation blind spots are scientifically well-documented**: women's ADHD symptoms fluctuate with hormonal cycles (estrogen-dopamine interaction confirmed by multiple studies), and high-functioning adults systematically score lower on screening tools.

3. **The supplement evidence hierarchy is clear**: ferritin and vitamin D deficiency warrant routine screening (especially given Czech deficiency rates above 75% in young adults), zinc has moderate meta-analytic support, while creatine, B6, and L-tyrosine remain theoretical for ADHD specifically.
